Yesterday at #TPAC2016 I heard several @W3C people saying that EME is needed because the web should be "inclusive", ie, those companies who want to add DRM'd content on the web should be able to do so in a W3C approved way, because - they say - otherwise those companies will either do it anyway (the current situation, via Flash or Silverlight) or do it outside the web (eg. via a standalone application). Their fear is that by not adding EME the W3C " will loose relevance".

This argument has been refuted before, for instance in the link I'm sharing here, and that I suggest you to read. But to highlight the rebuttal, here it is:

"The argument has been made that if the web doesn’t embrace this stuff, people won’t stop watching videos: they’ll just go somewhere other than the web to get them, and that is a correct argument. But what is the pointin bringing people to the web to watch their videos, if in order to do so the web becomes platform-specific and unopen and balkanised? The goal wasn’t “get everyone on the web”, it was “bring knowledge and entertainment and everything to everyone”, and the web was the best way to do that. If getting the “everything” means giving up on the “everyone”, we’re doing it wrong."

In my own words I'd say that while W3C fears it will lose some relevance if they don't approve EME, the general feeling outside W3C seems to be that they're losing a lot of relevance already, by shifting from being the builders of an open web to be just a vehicle to whatever companies might want from the web, at any cost.
